## C1. Chang & Li (2015): economics replication rates

Access date: 2026-06-21
Source: Federal Reserve Board, Finance and Economics Discussion
Series (FEDS) 2015-083, Andrew C. Chang and Phillip Li, "Is Economics
Research Replicable? Sixty Published Papers from Thirteen Journals Say
'Usually Not'." DOI 10.17016/FEDS.2015.083
URL: https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/is-economics-research-replicable-sixty-published-papers-from-thirteen-journals-say-quotusually-notquot.htm
Used in: Ch.1 (econ-primary evidence)

Supporting text (quoted verbatim from the Fed abstract page):
"We attempt to replicate 67 papers published in 13 well-regarded
economics journals using author-provided replication files that
include both data and code. ... Aside from 6 papers that use
confidential data, we obtain data and code replication files for 29
of 35 papers (83%) that are required to provide such files as a
condition of publication, compared to 11 of 26 papers (42%) that are
not required to provide data and code replication files. We
successfully replicate the key qualitative result of 22 of 67 papers
(33%) without contacting the authors. Excluding the 6 papers that use
confidential data and the 2 papers that use software we do not
possess, we replicate 29 of 59 papers (49%) with assistance from the
authors. Because we are able to replicate less than half of the
papers in our sample even with help from the authors, we assert that
economics research is usually not replicable."

Numbers cleared for use: 67 papers, 13 journals; 33% (22/67) without
author contact; 49% (29/59) with author assistance; data/code
availability 83% (required) vs 42% (not required).

Notes: Title variant exists. The Fed working paper says "Usually
Not"; the later peer-reviewed version (Critical Finance Review, now
publishers) says "Often Not". Cite the Fed FEDS paper (the primary,
freely accessible source) and the figures above, which match it.

## C2. Camerer et al. (2016): experimental economics replication

Access date: 2026-06-21
Source: Science 351(6280):1433-1436, 25 Mar 2016, Camerer et al.,
"Evaluating replicability of laboratory experiments in economics."
DOI 10.1126/science.aaf0918 ; PMID 26940865
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26940865/
Used in: Ch.1 (econ-primary evidence)

Supporting text (quoted verbatim from the PubMed abstract):
"To contribute data about replicability in economics, we replicated
18 studies published in the American Economic Review and the
Quarterly Journal of Economics between 2011 and 2014. ... We found a
significant effect in the same direction as in the original study for
11 replications (61%); on average, the replicated effect size is 66%
of the original."

Numbers cleared for use: 18 lab-experiment studies (AER + QJE,
2011-2014); 11/18 = 61% replicated significant same-direction;
replicated effect size 66% of original.

## C3. Open Science Collaboration (2015): psychology reproducibility

Access date: 2026-06-21
Source: Science 349(6251):aac4716, 28 Aug 2015, Open Science
Collaboration, "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological
science." DOI 10.1126/science.aac4716
URL: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aac4716
Used in: Ch.1 (broader-social-science context)

Supporting text (from the published abstract, verified via Science /
multiple authoritative summaries 2026-06-21): the project conducted
replications of 100 experimental and correlational studies from three
psychology journals; "Ninety-seven percent of original studies had
significant results (P < .05). Thirty-six percent of replications had
significant results." Replication effect sizes were about half the
magnitude of the originals.

Numbers cleared for use: 100 studies; 97% of originals significant
vs 36% of replications significant; replication effects ~half size.

Notes: Abstract numbers cross-checked against the Science DOI page and
the OSF project page (https://osf.io/ezcuj/). The science.org full
text is paywalled/JS-rendered; the abstract figures are stable and
widely quoted verbatim.

## C4. Camerer et al. (2018): social science replication (Nature/Science)

Access date: 2026-06-21
Source: Nature Human Behaviour 2:637-644, 2018, Camerer et al.,
"Evaluating the replicability of social science experiments in Nature
and Science between 2010 and 2015." DOI 10.1038/s41562-018-0399-z
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-018-0399-z
Used in: Ch.1 (broader-social-science context; bridges econ + psych)

Supporting text (from the published abstract, verified 2026-06-21):
replicated 21 systematically selected experimental social-science
studies published in Nature and Science between 2010 and 2015; found
"a significant effect in the same direction as the original study for
13 (62%) studies, and the effect size of the replications is on
average about 50% of the original effect size."

Numbers cleared for use: 21 studies (Nature + Science, 2010-2015);
13/21 = 62% replicated significant same-direction; replication effect
~50% of original.

## C5. AEA Data and Code Availability Policy + Data Editor role

Access date: 2026-06-21
Source: American Economic Association, "Data and Code Availability
Policy" (version February 2026).
URL: https://www.aeaweb.org/journals/data/data-code-policy
Used in: Ch.1 (why version control / reproducibility is now a career
skill: journal mandates), forward-ref Ch.15

Supporting text (quoted verbatim from the policy page):
"Prior to acceptance, authors of papers that contain empirical work,
simulations, or experimental work must provide the data, code, and
other details of the computations sufficient to permit replication.
These materials must be made available and retained in an openly
accessible trusted data repository, such as the AEA Data and Code
Repository."
And: "The AEA data editor will assess compliance with this policy,
including by conducting reproducibility checks and verifying the
accuracy of the information provided, and will assist the authors in
achieving compliance."

Additional (the "we run your code" requirement): the AEA Data Editor
guidance / FAQ states that, within reasonable limits of time and
computing resources, the Data Editor will run the author's code and
verify, prior to acceptance, that the results produced correspond to
the published results. Source: AEA Data and Code FAQ
(https://www.aeaweb.org/journals/data/faq) and the Office of the AEA
Data Editor guidance (https://aeadataeditor.github.io/). Verified via
search snippet 2026-06-21; in the book this is stated as paraphrase
attributed to the Data Editor's guidance, with the verbatim
"reproducibility checks" quote above as the anchor.

Cleared for use: AEA requires data + code sufficient to permit
replication, deposited in a trusted repository, BEFORE acceptance; a
dedicated AEA Data Editor runs reproducibility checks pre-acceptance.
The AEA endorses the cross-journal Data and Code Availability Standard
(DCAS v1.0, https://datacodestandard.org/).
